\title{A Delayed Renewal Cage Model for Transient Non-Gaussian Dynamics}

\author{Zijun Lu}
\affiliation{Independent research note}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
Glass-forming liquids and polymer melts often display a transient
non-Gaussian parameter (NGP) peak during the crossover from localized cage
motion to long-time transport. A harmonic Langevin cage explains the
mean-squared-displacement (MSD) plateau but remains Gaussian and therefore
cannot generate a nonzero NGP. Here we introduce a minimal delayed renewal
cage model in which local cage motion is interrupted by delayed discrete
cage-center renewal events, rather than by a continuously fluctuating
diffusivity. The model yields closed-form expressions for the MSD and NGP,
\begin{equation}
  \langle \Delta x^2(t) \rangle = L(t)+qR(t), \qquad
  \alpha_2(t)=\frac{q^2R(t)}{[L(t)+qR(t)]^2},
\end{equation}
where $L(t)$ is the local cage variance, $q$ is the jump variance per renewal
event, and $R(t)$ is the mean delayed renewal count. With renewal intensity
$r(t)=\lambda[1-\exp(-t/\tau_d)]^2$, the NGP starts from zero, develops a
finite-time maximum, and decays as $1/(\lambda t)$ at long times. In the
plateau regime the peak condition reduces to $qR(t^\ast)=A$, giving
$\alpha_2(t^\ast)=q/(4A)$. This also gives observable peak diagnostics,
including $q/A\simeq 4\alpha_2(t^\ast)$ and
$R(t^\ast)\simeq 1/[4\alpha_2(t^\ast)]$, plus a finite-time consistency check
that compares the peak-inferred renewal rate with a late-time NGP inversion.
The same renewal generator gives a closed-form self-intermediate scattering
function with a cage plateau and an alpha-relaxation rate. A minimal
temperature-dependent extension predicts when diffusion and structural
relaxation decouple through the product $\lambda(T)\tau_d(T)$ and gives a
closed time-temperature-superposition residual for the alpha shape. The delayed
renewal count regularizes the short-time behavior of jump models while
preserving analytic tractability and long-time Gaussian recovery. A
finite-exchange gamma extension then gives a closed criterion for
stretched-like alpha decay without leaving residual long-time non-Gaussianity,
and its predictions are contrasted with a static gamma-disorder null model. A
persistence-exchange extension separates first cage escape from subsequent
exchange, producing Stokes-Einstein decoupling at fixed long-time diffusion
while preserving Gaussian recovery. The result is an effective diagnostic
theory for dynamical glass signatures, with thermodynamic glass-transition
phenomena left as explicit closures rather than microscopic consequences of
the renewal dynamics.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Dynamic heterogeneity near glass transition is commonly visible through a
transient non-Gaussian parameter and broad tails in the self van Hove
distribution. A single harmonic Langevin cage gives an MSD plateau but remains
Gaussian. Conversely, diffusing-diffusivity and superstatistical models
generate non-Gaussian diffusion through fluctuating mobility
\cite{chubynsky2014diffusing,chechkin2017brownian,guan2014fickian}, and such
ideas have been tested in glass-forming liquids \cite{rusciano2022fickian},
with subsequent discussion about whether the Fickian non-Gaussian language is
the best description of supercooled-liquid heterogeneity
\cite{berthier2023comment,rusciano2022reply}. Cage rearrangements and
cage-size precursors are also directly visible in colloidal and simulated
glass-formers \cite{weeks2002cage,pastore2017cage}. The purpose of this note
is narrower: construct a closed-form renewal-cage model that connects local
caging, delayed cage rearrangement, an NGP peak, a self van Hove tail,
self-intermediate scattering relaxation, and long-time Gaussian recovery. The
non-Gaussianity here is not assigned to a continuously fluctuating diffusivity;
it is generated by the delayed, discrete renewal count of cage-center jumps.

\section{Model}

The displacement is decomposed into local cage motion plus cage-center jumps,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta x(t) = \xi_{\rm cage}(t) + \sum_{j=1}^{N(t)} \eta_j.
\end{equation}
The local cage contribution is Gaussian with variance
\begin{equation}
  L(t)=A\left[1-\exp(-t/\tau_c)\right].
\end{equation}
The cage-center jumps $\eta_j$ are independent Gaussian increments with
variance $q$. The renewal count $N(t)$ is an inhomogeneous Poisson process with
intensity
\begin{equation}
  r(t)=\lambda\left[1-\exp(-t/\tau_d)\right]^2.
\end{equation}
The corresponding mean renewal count is
\begin{equation}
  R(t)=\lambda\left[
    t-2\tau_d(1-e^{-t/\tau_d})
    +\frac{\tau_d}{2}(1-e^{-2t/\tau_d})
  \right].
\end{equation}
The delayed onset gives $R(t)\sim \lambda t^3/(3\tau_d^2)$ as $t\to0$.
The square in Eq.~(4) is not essential for the closed-form moment
calculation, but it is the minimal integer exponent that makes the NGP start
continuously from zero. More generally, if
$r_m(t)=\lambda[1-\exp(-t/\tau_d)]^m$, then
\begin{equation}
  R_m(t)\sim \frac{\lambda t^{m+1}}{(m+1)\tau_d^m},\qquad t\to0.
\end{equation}

\section{Moments and Non-Gaussian Parameter}

Conditional on $N(t)=n$, the displacement is Gaussian with variance
\begin{equation}
  V(t|n)=L(t)+nq.
\end{equation}
Since $N(t)$ is Poisson, $\mathbb{E}[V]=L+qR$ and
$\mathrm{Var}(V)=q^2R$. Thus
\begin{align}
  M_2(t) &= L(t)+qR(t),\\
  M_4(t) &= 3\left([L(t)+qR(t)]^2+q^2R(t)\right).
\end{align}
The one-dimensional NGP is
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)=\frac{M_4(t)}{3M_2(t)^2}-1
  =\frac{q^2R(t)}{[L(t)+qR(t)]^2}.
\end{equation}
For isotropic three-dimensional displacements, conditional Gaussianity gives
$\langle r^2|V\rangle=3V$ and $\langle r^4|V\rangle=15V^2$. Hence the standard
three-dimensional NGP,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2^{3D}(t)=\frac{3\langle r^4\rangle}{5\langle r^2\rangle^2}-1,
\end{equation}
reduces to the same expression.

\section{Self-Intermediate Scattering Function}

The same renewal structure gives a closed-form self-intermediate scattering
function. For a fixed renewal count, the displacement remains Gaussian, so
\begin{equation}
  F_s(k,t|N)=
  \exp\left[-\frac{k^2}{2}\left(L(t)+qN(t)\right)\right].
\end{equation}
Taking the Poisson average gives
\begin{equation}
  F_s(k,t)=
  \exp\left[
    -\frac{k^2L(t)}{2}
    +R(t)\left(e^{-k^2q/2}-1\right)
  \right].
\end{equation}
This separates the cage Debye-Waller factor from the renewal-controlled alpha
decay,
\begin{equation}
  F_s(k,t)=
  \exp[-k^2L(t)/2]\,
  \Phi_\alpha(k,t),
  \qquad
  \Phi_\alpha(k,t)=
  \exp\left[-\Gamma_k R(t)\right],
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma_k=1-e^{-k^2q/2}$. Thus the cage plateau is approximately
$f_k=\exp(-k^2A/2)$ once $L(t)\simeq A$ but before appreciable renewal, while
the long-time alpha-relaxation rate is
\begin{equation}
  \tau_\alpha(k)^{-1}\simeq
  \lambda\left(1-e^{-k^2q/2}\right).
\end{equation}
For small $k$, $\tau_\alpha(k)^{-1}\simeq \lambda k^2q/2$, matching ordinary
diffusive scaling with $D=\lambda q/2$ in one dimension. For larger $k$, the
rate saturates at $\lambda$, reflecting the renewal-count bottleneck rather
than a continuously varying diffusivity.
The same expression links the alpha time to the NGP peak without adding a
parameter. If the alpha time is defined by
$\Phi_\alpha(k,\tau_\alpha)=h$, then
\begin{equation}
  R_\alpha(k,h)
  =\frac{-\log h}{\Gamma_k},
  \qquad
  R_\ast=\frac{A}{q}.
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \frac{R_\alpha}{R_\ast}
  =
  \frac{-q\log h}
       {A\left(1-e^{-k^2q/2}\right)},
  \qquad
  \frac{\tau_\alpha}{t^\ast}
  =
  \frac{R^{-1}(R_\alpha)}{R^{-1}(R_\ast)} ,
\end{equation}
where the inverse is taken with the same delayed-renewal clock $R(t)$. This
gives a direct test of whether the NGP peak and structural relaxation are
controlled by one renewal process.

\section{Observable Inversion and Falsifiability}

The same formulas can be read in the reverse direction. Suppose a simulation or
experiment supplies the cage plateau $f_k$, long-time diffusion coefficient
$D$, cage-normalized alpha time $\tau_\alpha(k)$ at threshold $h$, and an
independent estimate of the delayed onset time $\tau_d$. Then
\begin{equation}
  A=-\frac{2\log f_k}{k^2}
\end{equation}
and $D=\lambda q/2$. The alpha-time equation gives
\begin{equation}
  \lambda\tau_dF(\tau_\alpha/\tau_d)
  \left(1-e^{-k^2q/2}\right)=-\log h.
\end{equation}
Eliminating $\lambda$ yields a scalar equation for $q$,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1-e^{-k^2q/2}}{q}=
  \frac{-\log h}{2D\tau_dF(\tau_\alpha/\tau_d)}.
\end{equation}
Because the left side is bounded above by $k^2/2$, the delayed-renewal model
has a positive-jump solution only if
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{M}_{\rm inv}\equiv
  \frac{D\tau_dF(\tau_\alpha/\tau_d)k^2}{-\log h}>1.
\end{equation}
This is a direct data-level falsification test: if the measured
$(D,\tau_\alpha,\tau_d,k)$ fail this inequality, no choice of positive jump
variance can make the minimal model reproduce both transport and scattering.
When the inequality holds, the inferred $q$ gives $\lambda=2D/q$, and the NGP
peak is no longer adjustable:
\begin{equation}
  R(t^\ast)=\frac{A}{q},\qquad
  \alpha_2(t^\ast)=\frac{q}{4A}.
\end{equation}
Thus the NGP peak time and height become out-of-sample checks on the parameters
inferred from $F_s(k,t)$ and long-time diffusion.

One can also invert the observables without supplying $\tau_d$ externally. Use
$f_k$ to infer $A$, the NGP peak height to infer
\begin{equation}
  q=4A\alpha_2(t^\ast),
\end{equation}
and diffusion to infer $\lambda=2D/q$. The peak time then fixes the delayed
onset by solving
\begin{equation}
  \lambda\tau_d F(t^\ast/\tau_d)=\frac{A}{q}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, with $s=t^\ast/\tau_d$,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{F(s)}{s}=\frac{A/q}{\lambda t^\ast}.
\end{equation}
The existence condition is $(A/q)/(\lambda t^\ast)<1$. After this inversion,
$\tau_\alpha(k)$ is a held-out consistency check rather than an input parameter.

\section{Temperature Dependence and Stokes-Einstein Decoupling}

The fixed-parameter model can be turned into a minimal temperature diagnostic
by assigning reduced-temperature dependence to its effective parameters. Let
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_T=\frac{1}{T}-\frac{1}{T_0}.
\end{equation}
We use the phenomenological laws
\begin{align}
  \lambda(T)&=\lambda_0 e^{-E_\lambda\Delta_T},&
  \tau_d(T)&=\tau_{d0} e^{E_d\Delta_T},\\
  A(T)&=A_0 e^{-E_A\Delta_T},&
  \frac{q(T)}{A(T)}&=\beta_0 e^{E_\beta\Delta_T}.
\end{align}
They encode four standard cooling trends without claiming a microscopic
derivation: renewal events become rarer, the delayed cage-breaking onset grows,
the local cage stiffens, and the jump-to-cage contrast may increase.

The long-time diffusion coefficient per coordinate is
\begin{equation}
  D(T)=\frac{\lambda(T)q(T)}{2}.
\end{equation}
The cage-normalized self-intermediate scattering function is
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_\alpha(k,t;T)=\exp[-\Gamma_k(T)R(t;T)].
\end{equation}
The $1/e$ alpha time is defined by
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_k(T)R(\tau_\alpha;T)=1,\qquad
  \Gamma_k(T)=1-e^{-k^2q(T)/2}.
\end{equation}
Using $R(t;T)=\lambda(T)\tau_d(T)F[t/\tau_d(T)]$ gives the closed diagnostic
\begin{equation}
  \tau_\alpha(k,T)=
  \tau_d(T)\,
  F^{-1}\left(
    \frac{1}{\Gamma_k(T)\lambda(T)\tau_d(T)}
  \right).
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}
  D(T)\tau_\alpha(k,T)=
  \frac{\lambda(T)q(T)\tau_d(T)}{2}\,
  F^{-1}\left(
    \frac{1}{\Gamma_k(T)\lambda(T)\tau_d(T)}
  \right).
\end{equation}
This expression is the model's Stokes-Einstein diagnostic. In the late-renewal
limit it reduces approximately to $q/(2\Gamma_k)$, so changing $\lambda$ alone
does not produce a strong violation. A violation appears when cooling increases
the delayed-onset product $\lambda(T)\tau_d(T)$ and/or the jump-to-cage contrast
fast enough that structural relaxation is bottlenecked by delayed cage renewal
more strongly than long-time diffusion.
The same scan gives the local fractional Stokes-Einstein exponent
\begin{equation}
  \xi_{\rm SE}(T)=
  -\frac{d\log D(T)}{d\log\tau_\alpha(k,T)},
  \qquad D\propto\tau_\alpha^{-\xi_{\rm SE}}.
\end{equation}
Ordinary Stokes-Einstein scaling corresponds to $\xi_{\rm SE}=1$, whereas
decoupled glassy transport gives $0<\xi_{\rm SE}<1$.
The apparent activation energy of the structural relaxation is
\begin{equation}
  E_{\rm app}(T)=
  \frac{d\log \tau_\alpha(k,T)}{d(1/T)}.
\end{equation}
For Arrhenius relaxation this is constant; growth of $E_{\rm app}$ on cooling
is the minimal fragility signal produced by the temperature-dependent delayed
renewal law. A local Angell-style fragility proxy is
$m_{\rm loc}(T)=E_{\rm app}(T)/(T\log 10)$.

The same equations give a closed diagnostic for time-temperature
superposition of the alpha relaxation. Define the scaled alpha-shape function
\begin{equation}
  Y_k(u;T)=
  \frac{-\log\Phi_\alpha(k,u\tau_\alpha;T)}{-\log h}.
\end{equation}
Using the delayed-renewal form gives
\begin{equation}
  Y_k(u;T)=
  \frac{\Gamma_k(T)\lambda(T)\tau_d(T)}
       {-\log h}
  F\left[u\,s_\alpha(T)\right],
  \qquad
  s_\alpha(T)=\frac{\tau_\alpha(k,T)}{\tau_d(T)}
  =
  F^{-1}\left[
    \frac{-\log h}{\Gamma_k(T)\lambda(T)\tau_d(T)}
  \right].
\end{equation}
Thus the alpha shape after scaling time by $\tau_\alpha$ is controlled by the
single variable
\begin{equation}
  C_k(T)=\Gamma_k(T)\lambda(T)\tau_d(T).
\end{equation}
Exact time-temperature superposition within this minimal model requires
$C_k(T)$ to be temperature independent. A data-level residual can be defined on
a chosen scaled-time grid $\{u_i\}$ as
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon_{\rm TTS}(T)=
  \left[
    \frac{1}{n}\sum_i
    \left(
      \log Y_k(u_i;T)-\log Y_k(u_i;T_{\rm ref})
    \right)^2
  \right]^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
This criterion is complementary to the Stokes-Einstein diagnostic: it tests the
shape of the alpha relaxation after removing its time scale, not only the
growth of $\tau_\alpha$.

\section{Activated-Barrier Interpretation and Dynamic Heterogeneity}

The temperature law can be read as a minimal activated-barrier reduction. If
the long-time renewal rate is controlled by a cage-breaking barrier
$E_\lambda$ while the delayed onset is controlled by a larger local persistence
barrier $E_d$, then
\begin{equation}
  \lambda(T)\tau_d(T)
  =\lambda_0\tau_{d0}\exp[(E_d-E_\lambda)\Delta_T].
\end{equation}
Thus the barrier gap $E_d-E_\lambda$ is the microscopic parameter that makes
the delayed-renewal control variable grow on cooling. This gives a direct
mechanism for the Stokes-Einstein diagnostic above: diffusion is set by the
asymptotic renewal rate and jump length, while structural relaxation is also
penalized by the delayed cage-breaking onset.

The same renewal-count fluctuations give a closed single-particle analogue of
the four-point susceptibility. Define
\begin{equation}
  W_k(t|N)=\exp[-k^2L(t)/2]a_k^{N(t)},\qquad
  a_k=e^{-k^2q/2}.
\end{equation}
Then $F_s(k,t)=\mathbb{E}[W_k(t|N)]$ and the renewal contribution to the
self-scattering variance is
\begin{equation}
  \chi_R(k,t)=\mathrm{Var}_N[W_k(t|N)]
  =e^{-k^2L(t)}
  \left[
    e^{R(t)(a_k^2-1)}-e^{2R(t)(a_k-1)}
  \right].
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\chi_R(k,t)}{F_s(k,t)^2}
  =\exp[R(t)(a_k-1)^2]-1.
\end{equation}
This is not a full spatial $\chi_4(t)$ because spatial correlations between
different particles have been integrated out. It is the renewal-count component:
it peaks when the number of cage renewals is most heterogeneous and can be
compared with the NGP and $F_s$ relaxation times in simulation trajectories.
To connect this single-particle renewal variance to a conventional four-point
susceptibility, suppose that a correlated renewal domain contains $N_{\rm corr}$
particles sharing the same renewal-count history. Then the renewal contribution
to the per-particle four-point susceptibility is
\begin{equation}
  \chi_4^R(k,t)=N_{\rm corr}\chi_R(k,t).
\end{equation}
Thus an observed renewal-dominated four-point peak gives the cooperative-size
estimator
\begin{equation}
  N_{\rm corr}=
  \frac{\chi_{4,\mathrm{peak}}^{\mathrm{obs}}}
       {\max_t \chi_R(k,t)}.
\end{equation}
This does not replace a spatial four-point calculation, but it gives a closed
conversion between the analytically predicted renewal fluctuation and a measured
dynamic-heterogeneity amplitude.
A minimal spatial closure can make $N_{\rm corr}$ clock-derived rather than
externally chosen. Suppose facilitation spreads diffusively during the local
persistence time $\tau_p$ with effective diffusivity $D_f$ in dimension $d$.
Then
\begin{equation}
  \xi_4(T)=\left[\ell_0^2+2dD_f\tau_p(T)\right]^{1/2},
  \qquad
  N_{\rm corr}(T)=\rho V_d\xi_4(T)^d,
\end{equation}
where $V_d=\pi^{d/2}/\Gamma(1+d/2)$ is the unit-ball volume and $\rho$ is the
number density. In the present delayed-renewal reduction we use
$\tau_p(T)\simeq\tau_d(T)$, so cooling that increases the persistence onset
also grows a dynamic length and hence the four-point amplitude. This is still a
compact-domain closure, not a microscopic spatial facilitation field, but it
moves the $\chi_4$ amplitude from an arbitrary scale factor to a falsifiable
clock-derived prediction.
The inverse test is equally simple:
\begin{equation}
  D_f(T)=\frac{\xi_4(T)^2-\ell_0^2}{2d\tau_p(T)}.
\end{equation}
Thus spatial data reject this closure if the $D_f(T)$ values inferred from
different temperatures do not collapse to a positive constant within
uncertainty. This gives a concrete four-point-length protocol without claiming
to derive the full spatial facilitation field.

\section{Thermodynamic Entropy Closure}

The renewal model above is kinetic. To compare with thermodynamic glass
signatures, one can add a minimal configurational-entropy sector rather than
pretending it was already present. Following the Kauzmann extrapolation
\cite{kauzmann1948nature}, take
\begin{equation}
  s_c(T)=s_{c0}\frac{T-T_K}{T_0-T_K},
\end{equation}
with $s_c(T_K)=0$. The corresponding excess heat capacity is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta c_p(T)=T\frac{ds_c}{dT}.
\end{equation}
The Adam-Gibbs relation \cite{adam1965temperature} then gives
\begin{equation}
  \tau_{\rm AG}(T)=\tau_0
  \exp\left[
    \frac{A_{\rm AG}}{T s_c(T)}
    -\frac{A_{\rm AG}}{T_0 s_c(T_0)}
  \right].
\end{equation}
In this closure, $\tau_{\rm AG}(T)$ sets the delayed-renewal persistence time
$\tau_d(T)$ while the renewal rate and cage parameters can be kept fixed or
combined with the dynamic laws above. This produces a thermodynamic route to
super-Arrhenius alpha slowdown and a Kauzmann-like entropy extrapolation. It is
still an added effective entropy law, not a microscopic derivation of
configurational entropy or a proof of an ideal glass transition; those are the
remaining RFOT-level questions
\cite{lubchenko2007theory,berthier2011theoretical}.
The accompanying non-identifiability certificate makes this boundary sharper:
two systems can share the same renewal observables while carrying different
entropy and heat-capacity closures, so the dynamical renewal data alone cannot
identify a Kauzmann closure or thermodynamic transition.

\section{Effective MCT Beta-Window Closure}

The cage plateau in the renewal model does not by itself reproduce the full
mode-coupling-theory beta-relaxation structure. To make this limitation
testable rather than hidden, we add a minimal beta-window envelope around a
normalized correlator plateau $f_c$,
\begin{equation}
  \phi_\beta(t)=
  \begin{cases}
    f_c+h_a(t/t_\beta)^{-a}, & t<t_\beta,\\
    f_c-h_b(t/t_\beta)^b, & t\ge t_\beta.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The first branch is the critical approach to the plateau and the second is the
von Schweidler departure \cite{gotze1992relaxation,kob1995intermediate}. In
idealized MCT the exponents satisfy a common exponent-parameter relation,
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_a=\frac{\Gamma(1-a)^2}{\Gamma(1-2a)},\qquad
  \lambda_b=\frac{\Gamma(1+b)^2}{\Gamma(1+2b)}.
\end{equation}
Therefore the mismatch $|\lambda_a-\lambda_b|$ is a direct diagnostic of
whether a fitted beta window is internally MCT-consistent. Cooling is encoded by
a beta-clock law $t_\beta(T)=t_{\beta0}\exp[E_\beta\Delta(1/T)]$ and by a
separate alpha time $\tau_\alpha(T)$, so the generated observable
$\tau_\alpha/t_\beta$ measures beta-alpha time-scale separation. This closure
upgrades the theory from merely having a cage plateau to reproducing the
two-sided beta-window power laws, but it remains an effective diagnostic
closure; the microscopic memory kernel and material-specific exponent parameter
are still outside the renewal derivation.

\section{Finite-Exchange Heterogeneity Extension}

The Poisson renewal model is the minimal clock, but glass-forming systems also
show trajectory-to-trajectory mobility heterogeneity. Before introducing
exchange, it is useful to state the static mobility-disorder null model. Let a
trajectory have a fixed dimensionless mobility $M$ drawn from a gamma
distribution with mean one and shape $\kappa_0$, and let
$N(t)|M$ be Poisson with mean $MR(t)$. Then
\begin{equation}
  G_N^{\rm st}(z;t)=
  \left[1+\frac{R(t)(1-z)}{\kappa_0}\right]^{-\kappa_0},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}N(t)=R(t),\qquad
  {\rm Var}\,N(t)=R(t)+\frac{R(t)^2}{\kappa_0}.
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2^{\rm st}(t)=
  \frac{q^2[R(t)+R(t)^2/\kappa_0]}{[L(t)+qR(t)]^2}
  \longrightarrow \frac{1}{\kappa_0},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_\alpha^{\rm st}(k,t)=
  \left[1+\frac{\Gamma_kR(t)}{\kappa_0}\right]^{-\kappa_0},
  \qquad
  -\frac{\log\Phi_\alpha^{\rm st}}{R(t)}\longrightarrow0.
\end{equation}
Thus static gamma disorder can broaden alpha relaxation, but it predicts a
nonzero long-time NGP plateau and an alpha decay that becomes subexponential in
renewal count. It therefore cannot explain stretched-like alpha relaxation and
long-time Gaussian recovery simultaneously.

A minimal closed correction is to let the renewal count be gamma mixed over a
finite exchange window. Conditional
on a dimensionless mobility $M$, take $N(t)|M$ to be Poisson with mean
$MR(t)$, and let the gamma shape self-average as
\begin{equation}
  \kappa_{\rm eff}(t)=
  \kappa_0\left[1+\frac{R(t)}{R_x}\right].
\end{equation}
The renewal-count probability-generating function is then the negative-binomial
form
\begin{equation}
  G_N(z;t)=
  \left[
    1+\frac{R(t)(1-z)}{\kappa_{\rm eff}(t)}
  \right]^{-\kappa_{\rm eff}(t)} .
\end{equation}
It preserves the mean renewal count but adds finite-time overdispersion,
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}N(t)=R(t),\qquad
  {\rm Var}\,N(t)=R(t)+\frac{R(t)^2}{\kappa_{\rm eff}(t)}.
\end{equation}
Since $\kappa_{\rm eff}(t)\sim \kappa_0R(t)/R_x$ at long times, the relative
count variance still decays as $1/R(t)$ and Gaussian recovery is retained.
The NGP and cage-normalized alpha decay become
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2^{\rm hx}(t)=
  \frac{q^2{\rm Var}\,N(t)}
       {[L(t)+qR(t)]^2},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_\alpha^{\rm hx}(k,t)=
  G_N(e^{-k^2q/2};t)
  =
  \left[
    1+\frac{\Gamma_kR(t)}{\kappa_{\rm eff}(t)}
  \right]^{-\kappa_{\rm eff}(t)} .
\end{equation}
The local stretching diagnostic is
\begin{equation}
  \beta_{\rm loc}(t)=
  \frac{d\log[-\log\Phi_\alpha^{\rm hx}(k,t)]}{d\log t}.
\end{equation}
Values $\beta_{\rm loc}<1$ in the alpha window are therefore produced by finite
exchange of renewal mobility, while the same construction predicts the
long-time return of $\alpha_2(t)$ toward zero.
For a window-level comparison with the experimental stretched-exponential
language, we also fit
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_\alpha^{\rm hx}(k,t)\simeq
  \exp\left[-(t/\tau_{\rm KWW})^{\beta_{\rm KWW}}\right],
\end{equation}
by regressing $\log[-\log\Phi_\alpha]$ against $\log t$ over a fixed alpha
decay window. This produces a falsifiable triple
$(\beta_{\rm KWW},\tau_{\rm KWW},\epsilon_{\rm KWW})$ rather than a qualitative
claim of stretching.
It also gives two late-time consistency tests governed by the same ratio
$c=R_x/\kappa_0$:
\begin{equation}
  R(t)\alpha_2^{\rm hx}(t)\to 1+c,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  -\frac{\log\Phi_\alpha^{\rm hx}(k,t)}{R(t)}
  \to
  \frac{\log(1+\Gamma_k c)}{c}.
\end{equation}
Thus a late NGP amplitude estimates $c$ as $c=R\alpha_2-1$, while the long-time
alpha slope independently estimates the same $c$ by inverting
$\log(1+\Gamma_kc)/c$. Agreement between these two estimates is a falsifiable
signature of finite-exchange heterogeneity rather than static mobility
disorder.
This also defines an experimental diagnostic map. For each wave number, the
pair $(R\alpha_2,\,-\log\Phi_\alpha/R)$ places a trajectory ensemble on a
one-parameter curve indexed by $c$. Large $c$ simultaneously increases the late
NGP amplitude and slows the alpha decay per renewal; if the two inferred values
of $c$ disagree, the finite-exchange renewal mechanism is insufficient.
In practice, late-time data give the two estimates
\begin{equation}
  c_{\rm NGP}=R_\ell\alpha_2(t_\ell)-1,
  \qquad
  \frac{\log(1+\Gamma_k c_\alpha)}{c_\alpha}
  =
  -\frac{\log\Phi_\alpha(k,t_\ell)}{R_\ell}.
\end{equation}
The scalar residual
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_c=\log(c_\alpha/c_{\rm NGP})
\end{equation}
is therefore a direct finite-exchange falsification statistic. Small
$|\Delta_c|$ supports the one-parameter exchange picture; a large residual
shows that NGP recovery and alpha slowing are not controlled by the same renewal
heterogeneity.
With independent uncertainties in $R_\ell$, $\alpha_2(t_\ell)$, and
$s_\alpha=-\log\Phi_\alpha/R_\ell$, the same protocol gives a statistical
score. The propagated NGP uncertainty is
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{c_{\rm NGP}}^2
  =
  \alpha_2(t_\ell)^2\sigma_R^2+R_\ell^2\sigma_{\alpha_2}^2,
\end{equation}
while $c_\alpha$ uses
\begin{equation}
  f'(c)=
  \frac{\Gamma_k c/(1+\Gamma_kc)-\log(1+\Gamma_kc)}{c^2},
  \qquad
  \sigma_{c_\alpha}=\frac{\sigma_{s_\alpha}}{|f'(c_\alpha)|}.
\end{equation}
Thus
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_{\Delta_c}^2=
  \left(\frac{\sigma_{c_\alpha}}{c_\alpha}\right)^2+
  \left(\frac{\sigma_{c_{\rm NGP}}}{c_{\rm NGP}}\right)^2,\qquad
  z_c=\frac{|\Delta_c|}{\sigma_{\Delta_c}}.
\end{equation}
The same construction is overdetermined when $F_s(k,t)$ is measured at several
wave numbers. Each $k$ gives an independent $c_\alpha(k)$ through
$\log(1+\Gamma_k c)/c=s_\alpha(k)$, while the late NGP gives one
$c_{\rm NGP}$. A valid finite-exchange renewal description therefore requires
the set $\{c_\alpha(k)\}$ to collapse to the same value and to agree with
$c_{\rm NGP}$ within propagated errors.

\section{Persistence-Exchange Decoupling}

The delayed Poisson clock above captures a smooth onset, but glassy dynamics
often separates the first persistence time from the subsequent exchange time
\cite{hedges2007persistence}.
To isolate that effect, let the first cage escape have mean $\tau_p$ and all
later exchanges have mean $\tau_x$. For $n\ge1$ the renewal-count distribution
can be written as
\begin{equation}
  P[N(t)=n]=
  \int_0^t \frac{e^{-(t-u)/\tau_p}}{\tau_p}
  e^{-u/\tau_x}\frac{(u/\tau_x)^{n-1}}{(n-1)!}\,du,
  \qquad
  P[N(t)=0]=e^{-t/\tau_p}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the count probability-generating function is closed:
\begin{equation}
  G(z,t)=\langle z^{N(t)}\rangle
  =e^{-t/\tau_p}
  +\frac{z\tau_p^{-1}\left[
    e^{(z-1)t/\tau_x}-e^{-t/\tau_p}
  \right]}
  {\tau_p^{-1}+(z-1)\tau_x^{-1}},
\end{equation}
where the removable singularity is evaluated by continuity. With
$a_k=e^{-k^2q/2}$ this gives the cage-normalized alpha relaxation directly as
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_\alpha^{px}(k,t)=G(a_k,t),
\end{equation}
and the NGP remains a count-variance diagnostic,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2^{px}(t)=
  \frac{q^2{\rm Var}\,N(t)}
       {[L(t)+q\langle N(t)\rangle]^2}.
\end{equation}
This extension can also be inverted from directly measurable dynamical
quantities. If $q$ is fixed from the MSD jump scale or from the preceding
peak/scattering inversion, long-time diffusion gives
\begin{equation}
  \tau_x=\frac{q}{2D}.
\end{equation}
The first-persistence time is then the solution of the scalar equation
\begin{equation}
  G(a_k,\tau_\alpha;\tau_p,\tau_x)=h,
\end{equation}
with $\tau_p\ge\tau_x$. This inversion uses $D$ and the cage-normalized
alpha time only. A late NGP measurement is therefore held out:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_{\rm px}=
  \log\frac{\alpha_{2,\mathrm{obs}}(t_\ell)}
           {\alpha_{2,\mathrm{pred}}^{px}(t_\ell)}.
\end{equation}
Small $|\Delta_{\rm px}|$ supports a persistence/exchange interpretation,
whereas a large residual rejects it even if the same $D\tau_\alpha$ product can
be matched.
The Poisson renewal model is recovered when $\tau_p=\tau_x$. When
$\tau_p>\tau_x$, the long-time diffusion coefficient remains
$D=q/(2\tau_x)$, but the cage-normalized alpha relaxation is delayed by the
first-persistence clock. Thus $D\tau_\alpha$ grows with $\tau_p/\tau_x$ even at
fixed diffusion. This gives a sharper Stokes-Einstein diagnostic than changing
one renewal rate: alpha relaxation probes first escape, while transport probes
the post-escape exchange clock. Because $\langle N(t)\rangle\sim t/\tau_x$ and
${\rm Var}\,N(t)/\langle N(t)\rangle^2\to0$, the long-time NGP still recovers
to zero.

\section{Asymptotics and Peak Condition}

At short times,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)\sim
  \frac{q^2\lambda\tau_c^2}{3A^2\tau_d^2}t.
\end{equation}
For the generalized delay exponent $m$ introduced above,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)\sim
  \frac{q^2\lambda\tau_c^2}{(m+1)A^2\tau_d^m}t^{m-1}.
\end{equation}
Thus $m<1$ would give a singular origin, $m=1$ a finite nonzero NGP at the
origin, and $m>1$ a regular zero-origin NGP. The present $m=2$ choice is the
smallest integer member of this regular class and yields the linear onset in
Eq.~(12).
At long times $L(t)\to A$ and $R(t)\sim \lambda t$, so
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)\sim \frac{1}{\lambda t}.
\end{equation}
Differentiating the exact expression gives the peak condition
\begin{equation}
  R'(t)\left[L(t)-qR(t)\right]-2R(t)L'(t)=0.
\end{equation}
In the plateau regime this reduces to
\begin{equation}
  qR(t^\ast)=A,\qquad \alpha_2(t^\ast)=\frac{q}{4A}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, a measured peak height gives the plateau-regime estimates
\begin{equation}
  \frac{q}{A}\simeq 4\alpha_2(t^\ast),\qquad
  R(t^\ast)\simeq \frac{1}{4\alpha_2(t^\ast)}.
\end{equation}
For the delayed intensity used above, write
\begin{equation}
  R(t)=\lambda\tau_d F(t/\tau_d),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  F(s)=s-2(1-e^{-s})+\frac{1}{2}(1-e^{-2s}).
\end{equation}
If $\tau_d$ is obtained independently or fitted from the rise of the peak, the
peak time estimates the asymptotic renewal rate,
\begin{equation}
  \lambda \simeq
  \frac{1}{4\alpha_2(t^\ast)\tau_d F(t^\ast/\tau_d)}.
\end{equation}
The independent asymptotic long-time check is
$\lambda\simeq[t\,\alpha_2(t)]^{-1}$ once $t$ is beyond the cage plateau and
renewal delay. A finite-time version is obtained without taking the
$t\to\infty$ limit. Let
\begin{equation}
  \beta=\frac{q}{A}\simeq 4\alpha_2(t^\ast),\qquad y(t)=\beta R(t).
\end{equation}
Then the plateau NGP can be written as
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)=\frac{\beta y(t)}{[1+y(t)]^2},
\end{equation}
which has maximum $\beta/4$ at $y=1$. Therefore any value used in this
finite-time inversion must satisfy $\alpha_\ell\le \beta/4$. For
$0<\alpha_\ell<\beta/4$, the inverse has two branches,
\begin{equation}
  y_{\pm}=
  \frac{\beta-2\alpha_\ell\pm\sqrt{\beta^2-4\beta\alpha_\ell}}
       {2\alpha_\ell},
\end{equation}
with $y_-y_+=1$. The pre-peak branch has $y_-<1$, while the post-peak branch
has $y_+>1$. Thus a late-time NGP value $\alpha_\ell=\alpha_2(t_\ell)$ gives
\begin{equation}
  y_\ell=
  \frac{\beta-2\alpha_\ell+\sqrt{\beta^2-4\beta\alpha_\ell}}
       {2\alpha_\ell},
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_\ell\simeq
  \frac{y_\ell}{\beta\tau_d F(t_\ell/\tau_d)}.
\end{equation}
Agreement between $\lambda_\ell$ and the peak-inferred rate is a falsifiable
consistency condition using only the peak height, peak time, delay scale, and a
late NGP value.

\section{Self Van Hove Distribution}

The three-dimensional radial self van Hove distribution is a Poisson-weighted
Maxwell mixture,
\begin{equation}
  G_s(r,t)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty P[N(t)=n]
  \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\frac{r^2}{V_n(t)^{3/2}}
  \exp\left[-\frac{r^2}{2V_n(t)}\right],
\end{equation}
with $V_n(t)=L(t)+nq$. This representation shows directly why the distribution
has transient broad tails near the NGP peak and returns toward a matched
Gaussian baseline at long times.

observables, not particle-resolved waiting and exchange events, and they still
lack physical time semantics. The canary therefore tightens the next target
from ``get more data'' to ``extract particle-resolved cage-jump events with a
physical clock'' \cite{weeks2002cage,pastore2017cage,hedges2007persistence}.

The same distinction is enforced for the event-clock threshold robustness test.
The package includes a GlassBench threshold-readiness ledger showing that the
KA2D archive exposes the required particle-coordinate schema and short-window
member-ensemble observables. The companion first-NPZ particle-cache manifest
uses bounded HTTP range reads to persist the two first-NPZ coordinate caches and
verifies their NPZ MD5 hashes and coordinate shape \(20\times1290\times2\).
The real threshold-robustness claim remains blocked, however, because those
cached coordinates still lack a physical-time trajectory axis,
threshold-sweep event clocks, and held-out macro observables.
The cached-particle time-code bridge narrows this statement: the cached first
NPZ files now have official physical lag times attached, but their leading
coordinate axis is an isoconfigurational-replica axis rather than a physical
time axis. Hence they support fixed-lag particle observables, not yet a
particle event-clock trajectory. A structure-matched multi-lag target manifest
therefore lists the exact official NPZ members and hashes that must be cached
before this fixed-lag evidence can become a particle-level event-clock test.
For the cold KA2D \(T=0.23\) structure-151 ladder, extended bounded-prefix
extraction now caches all eight official lag targets. This closes the
structure-matched particle-cache gap for that ladder, but it still does not by
itself create a physical-time event-clock trajectory. The cached-coordinate
observable audit now uses the cached \texttt{initial\_positions} arrays to
recompute minimal-image displacement observables. For all eight cold
structure-151 lag targets, the recomputed MSD reproduces the official
GlassBench MSD to numerical precision. The same audit also resolves the NGP
averaging convention: the official two-dimensional NGP is the mean over
isoconfigurational replicas of each replica's alpha-two value, not a pooled
ratio over all replicas and particles. With that convention the cached
displacements reproduce the official NGP as well, while the raw coordinate MSD
and pooled NGP remain rejected proxies. Finally, the same displacement tensor
reproduces the official multi-\(k\) self-intermediate scattering when
\(F_s(k,t)\) is computed as the average of the \(x\)- and \(y\)-axis cosine
projections; a single-axis convention is rejected by the audit. Thus the
archive-to-observable semantic gap is closed for MSD, NGP, and multi-\(k\)
\(F_s\) on this fixed-lag ladder; the remaining promotion blockers are event
segmentation, persistence/exchange clocks, and alpha-threshold coverage, not
fixed-lag observable reproducibility.
The same fixed-lag ladder already rejects one tempting shortcut. If the
structure-151 lag codes are treated as a single delayed-renewal event clock,
the plateau-plus-renewal moment equations imply a lag-dependent effective jump
variance \(q_{\rm eff}\). In the real \(T=0.23\) ladder, \(q_{\rm eff}\) has
coefficient of variation \(1.76\) and spans a factor \(899\), while the same
rows show a large NGP peak \(7.27\) and multi-\(k\) scattering decay. Thus the
real coordinate data do not merely wait for a fit; they already reject the
naive lag-clock closure and require particle-level cage-jump event
segmentation before persistence/exchange inversion.

The next convergence test is stricter: it asks whether the real trajectory
statistics can already be used as microscopic inputs for held-out
macro-dynamical predictions. The current answer is useful but negative. The
GlassBench member-ensemble probe gives real frame-index microstatistics,
including a cage-length proxy, short-frame NGP, short-frame multi-\(k\)
scattering decay, and member uncertainties. The corrected time-code curve
gives real macro dynamical signatures. However, these two evidence streams are
not yet a closed loop because the frame-index trajectory probe has no physical
time semantics, no cage-jump event segmentation, no persistence/exchange event
clock, and the anchor alpha definition is still inconsistent with the metadata
\(\tau_\alpha\). We therefore treat the real data as supporting the diagnostic
direction, but not yet as a successful microstatistics-to-held-out-prediction
test.

The trajectory-adapter contract

temperature or state point, species labels, and units metadata. The GlassBench
KA and KA2D rows are therefore kept at the remote-contract stage with
\texttt{coordinate\_file} as the first blocker, while a synthetic local row

not a claim that real GlassBench trajectories have already been fitted.
The companion dry-run file
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_trajectory\_adapter\_demo.csv} then exercises the local
adapter path on a deterministic particle table: rows with frame, time,
particle identity, and coordinate columns are sorted into a
frame--particle--coordinate tensor before the MSD, NGP, self-intermediate
scattering, and overlap-$\chi_4$ extraction is run. This makes the remaining
GlassBench step a concrete column/path mapping problem for an extracted archive,

The file-level dry run

\texttt{renewal\_cage\_trajectory\_csv\_adapter\_source.csv}, then reloaded by

and unit metadata checks before the same observable extraction is applied.
The bridge file
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_trajectory\_curve\_bridge.csv} then compresses
trajectory lag rows into the raw-curve quantities required for inversion:
diffusion from the late MSD slope proxy, late NGP, alpha-crossing times for
each wave number, the anchor $D\tau_\alpha$ product, and the overlap-$\chi_4$
peak. It also records a negative short-trajectory case where the observable
rows exist but $F_s(k,t)$ has not crossed the alpha threshold, so the row is
kept out of the inversion gate rather than overclaimed.
The downstream file

further by feeding a bridge-ready row into the persistence/exchange joint

short-trajectory row remains blocked at the alpha-threshold-crossing gate.

fit inputs from predictions by using the inferred persistence/exchange clocks
to score an unfitted alpha wave number and a later NGP point. This is the
trajectory-level version of the cross-observable prediction ledger: a real
SOTA trajectory comparison should pass these held-out rows, not merely fit the
calibration curves.
The companion file
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_trajectory\_prediction\_falsification.csv} converts
those held-out scores into protocol-level outcomes: one row is
falsification-ready and passes, one row is blocked by upstream alpha-threshold
coverage, and a fit-only negative control is explicitly marked as an overclaim
risk.

$\tau_\alpha(k)$ as calibration inputs, can the inferred persistence/exchange

growth, and the $\chi_4$ proxy as held-out dynamical checks? The synthetic

\texttt{thermodynamic\_claim\_allowed=0}, preserving the distinction between a

intermittent-trajectory example is not a real-data fit; it verifies that the

accepting a finite threshold window and rejecting both over-segmented and
under-segmented event clocks.

trajectory-derived observables. Its columns
\texttt{sigma\_msd}, \texttt{sigma\_ngp}, \texttt{sigma\_self\_intermediate\_scattering},

downstream raw-curve and persistence/exchange protocols.

observables are averaged only into standard-error rows, not into a completed
real-data fit. This is the route required for a future GlassBench-style
multi-\texttt{npz} reanalysis once physical time units and enough independent
members are available.
For the current GlassBench first-\texttt{npz} rows, the separate
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_sota\_glassbench\_observable\_coverage\_audit.csv}

single-origin overlap-\(\chi_4\) proxy, but not physical lag time. The audit
also forbids using \texttt{rhomax} or machine-learning feature curves as
substitutes for those dynamical observables in later comparisons, so the
current GlassBench evidence remains a coordinate-level canary rather than an
uncertainty-weighted real inversion.
The companion
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_sota\_glassbench\_first\_npz\_structural\_observable\_plan.csv}

\texttt{positions.npy}, \texttt{box.npy}, and \texttt{types.npy}, and the
implemented trajectory protocol can compute MSD, NGP, multi-\(k\)
self-intermediate scattering, and overlap-\(\chi_4\). The current row records
derived structural observables without retaining raw coordinate bytes, so its
remaining blocker is physical lag-time semantics rather than coordinate
availability.
The gate
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_trajectory\_inversion\_readiness.csv} then separates a
trajectory source that is merely structurally usable from one that is ready for
uncertainty-weighted inversion. Promotion requires the required observable set,
a shared lag grid, shared particle identity, and positive uncertainty columns;
the structural-only example is deliberately blocked by the missing
\texttt{sigma\_*} fields, while both the time-origin and independent-member
synthetic canaries reach the uncertainty-weighted protocol stage.
The publication ladder
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_benchmark\_publication\_ladder.csv} then collapses the
metadata, readiness, and held-out prediction gates into manuscript-safe claim
levels. The current GlassBench row is allowed only as
\texttt{metadata\_readiness\_only}; the synthetic trajectory canary is allowed
as \texttt{protocol\_canary\_passed} but not as a real-data fit; and the
fit-only negative control is blocked as \texttt{do\_not\_claim\_prediction}.
This is the publication guard that separates a reproducible inversion
protocol from a completed SOTA trajectory reanalysis.
Finally,
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_raw\_curve\_ingestion\_contract.csv} specifies the
column-level contract for turning the structurally valid Kob-Andersen I/II
fusion into an actual uncertainty-weighted inversion: self-intermediate
scattering requires \texttt{temperature}, \texttt{wave\_number}, \texttt{time},
and \texttt{F\_s} plus \texttt{sigma\_F\_s}; the van-Hove/NGP input requires
\texttt{G\_s}, $\alpha_2$, diffusion, and their uncertainties. Thus the paper
does not treat digitized curves as a full real-data fit until the uncertainty
columns are supplied.
The file
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_raw\_curve\_diagnostic\_readiness.csv} then aggregates
these observable-level contracts to the diagnostic level, so that the multi-$k$
alpha-shape, van-Hove recovery, and combined alpha/van-Hove/transport closure
each carry separate structural-readiness and uncertainty-readiness flags.
The companion
\texttt{renewal\_cage\_raw\_curve\_persistence\_exchange\_protocol.csv}
demonstrates the full synthetic-to-diagnostic path: machine-readable
$F_s(k,t)$ curves are reduced to $\tau_\alpha(k)$ by threshold crossing, the

same persistence/exchange clocks must then pass the multi-$k$, late-NGP,
Stokes-Einstein, and $\chi_4$ tests. A deliberately corrupted late-NGP curve is
rejected without refitting the clocks.

\section{Discussion}

The model is not intended to replace molecular simulations or microscopic cage
jump analysis. Its role is to isolate a minimal mechanism: delayed renewal of
local cages creates a finite-time NGP peak while preserving ordinary long-time
Gaussian recovery. The delay time $\tau_d$ mainly controls when the peak
appears; the ratio $q/A$ controls its height in the plateau regime. This
separation of roles may be useful when interpreting short-time simulations or
coarse-grained cage-jump statistics near glass transition.
The generalized exponent argument also makes a falsifiable modeling
assumption explicit: a measured early-time NGP scaling
$\alpha_2(t)\propto t^\beta$ corresponds to an effective renewal-delay
exponent $m=\beta+1$ within this class. The square-delay model therefore
predicts an initially linear NGP rise, not merely a finite peak. The
self-intermediate scattering result adds an independent glass-literature
observable: $\tau_\alpha(k)$ is controlled by the same renewal rate that appears
in the NGP consistency check. The temperature extension adds a separate
criterion for Stokes-Einstein violation: changing $\lambda(T)$ alone mainly
rescales both diffusion and relaxation, while growth of $\tau_d(T)$ relative to
$1/\lambda(T)$ produces decoupling through the inverse renewal shape $F^{-1}$.
The activated-barrier interpretation makes this criterion sharper:
$E_d>E_\lambda$ is the condition for $\lambda(T)\tau_d(T)$ to grow on cooling.

Several assumptions are deliberately restrictive. Renewal events are taken to
be Poisson once the delayed intensity is specified; cage-center jumps are
Gaussian and uncorrelated; and the local cage variance is reduced to a single
relaxation scale. These choices are what make the moment hierarchy and van Hove
mixture analytic. Their role is diagnostic rather than microscopic: correlated
jumps, broad waiting-time distributions, or non-Gaussian local cage motion would
modify the peak shape, but they would have to reproduce the same four
observables--the MSD plateau, the early-time NGP exponent, the peak/late-time
renewal-rate consistency, and the long-time $1/t$ decay--to fall in the same
renewal-cage universality class.

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} summarizes the distinction from nearby modeling
languages. The point is not that a renewal model is the only explanation of a
non-Gaussian peak. Rather, the delayed renewal construction supplies an
explicit bridge between a cage plateau, a finite-time NGP maximum, a radial van
Hove tail, the early-time exponent, and peak/late-time diagnostics that can be
checked against simulation trajectories.
