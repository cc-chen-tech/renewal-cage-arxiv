\title{A Delayed Renewal Cage Model for Transient Non-Gaussian Dynamics}

\author{Zijun Lu}
\affiliation{Independent research note}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
Glass-forming liquids and polymer melts often display a transient
non-Gaussian parameter (NGP) peak during the crossover from localized cage
motion to long-time transport. A harmonic Langevin cage explains the
mean-squared-displacement (MSD) plateau but remains Gaussian and therefore
cannot generate a nonzero NGP. Here we introduce a minimal delayed renewal
cage model in which local cage motion is interrupted by delayed cage-center
renewal events. The model yields closed-form expressions for the MSD and NGP,
\begin{equation}
  \langle \Delta x^2(t) \rangle = L(t)+qR(t), \qquad
  \alpha_2(t)=\frac{q^2R(t)}{[L(t)+qR(t)]^2},
\end{equation}
where $L(t)$ is the local cage variance, $q$ is the jump variance per renewal
event, and $R(t)$ is the mean delayed renewal count. With renewal intensity
$r(t)=\lambda[1-\exp(-t/\tau_d)]^2$, the NGP starts from zero, develops a
finite-time maximum, and decays as $1/(\lambda t)$ at long times. In the
plateau regime the peak condition reduces to $qR(t^\ast)=A$, giving
$\alpha_2(t^\ast)=q/(4A)$. This also gives observable peak diagnostics,
including $q/A\simeq 4\alpha_2(t^\ast)$ and
$R(t^\ast)\simeq 1/[4\alpha_2(t^\ast)]$. The delayed renewal count regularizes
the short-time behavior of jump models while preserving analytic tractability
and long-time Gaussian recovery.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Dynamic heterogeneity near glass transition is commonly visible through a
transient non-Gaussian parameter and broad tails in the self van Hove
distribution. A single harmonic Langevin cage gives an MSD plateau but remains
Gaussian. Conversely, diffusing-diffusivity and superstatistical models
generate non-Gaussian diffusion through fluctuating mobility
\cite{chubynsky2014diffusing,chechkin2017brownian,guan2014fickian}, and such
ideas have been tested in glass-forming liquids \cite{rusciano2022fickian},
with subsequent discussion about whether the Fickian non-Gaussian language is
the best description of supercooled-liquid heterogeneity
\cite{berthier2023comment,rusciano2022reply}. Cage rearrangements and
cage-size precursors are also directly visible in colloidal and simulated
glass-formers \cite{weeks2002cage,pastore2017cage}. The purpose of this note
is narrower: construct a closed-form renewal-cage model that connects local
caging, delayed cage rearrangement, an NGP peak, and long-time Gaussian
recovery without claiming that glassy dynamics is simply a fluctuating
diffusivity process.

\section{Model}

The displacement is decomposed into local cage motion plus cage-center jumps,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta x(t) = \xi_{\rm cage}(t) + \sum_{j=1}^{N(t)} \eta_j.
\end{equation}
The local cage contribution is Gaussian with variance
\begin{equation}
  L(t)=A\left[1-\exp(-t/\tau_c)\right].
\end{equation}
The cage-center jumps $\eta_j$ are independent Gaussian increments with
variance $q$. The renewal count $N(t)$ is an inhomogeneous Poisson process with
intensity
\begin{equation}
  r(t)=\lambda\left[1-\exp(-t/\tau_d)\right]^2.
\end{equation}
The corresponding mean renewal count is
\begin{equation}
  R(t)=\lambda\left[
    t-2\tau_d(1-e^{-t/\tau_d})
    +\frac{\tau_d}{2}(1-e^{-2t/\tau_d})
  \right].
\end{equation}
The delayed onset gives $R(t)\sim \lambda t^3/(3\tau_d^2)$ as $t\to0$.
The square in Eq.~(4) is not essential for the closed-form moment
calculation, but it is the minimal integer exponent that makes the NGP start
continuously from zero. More generally, if
$r_m(t)=\lambda[1-\exp(-t/\tau_d)]^m$, then
\begin{equation}
  R_m(t)\sim \frac{\lambda t^{m+1}}{(m+1)\tau_d^m},\qquad t\to0.
\end{equation}

\section{Moments and Non-Gaussian Parameter}

Conditional on $N(t)=n$, the displacement is Gaussian with variance
\begin{equation}
  V(t|n)=L(t)+nq.
\end{equation}
Since $N(t)$ is Poisson, $\mathbb{E}[V]=L+qR$ and
$\mathrm{Var}(V)=q^2R$. Thus
\begin{align}
  M_2(t) &= L(t)+qR(t),\\
  M_4(t) &= 3\left([L(t)+qR(t)]^2+q^2R(t)\right).
\end{align}
The one-dimensional NGP is
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)=\frac{M_4(t)}{3M_2(t)^2}-1
  =\frac{q^2R(t)}{[L(t)+qR(t)]^2}.
\end{equation}
For isotropic three-dimensional displacements, conditional Gaussianity gives
$\langle r^2|V\rangle=3V$ and $\langle r^4|V\rangle=15V^2$. Hence the standard
three-dimensional NGP,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2^{3D}(t)=\frac{3\langle r^4\rangle}{5\langle r^2\rangle^2}-1,
\end{equation}
reduces to the same expression.

\section{Asymptotics and Peak Condition}

At short times,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)\sim
  \frac{q^2\lambda\tau_c^2}{3A^2\tau_d^2}t.
\end{equation}
For the generalized delay exponent $m$ introduced above,
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)\sim
  \frac{q^2\lambda\tau_c^2}{(m+1)A^2\tau_d^m}t^{m-1}.
\end{equation}
Thus $m<1$ would give a singular origin, $m=1$ a finite nonzero NGP at the
origin, and $m>1$ a regular zero-origin NGP. The present $m=2$ choice is the
smallest integer member of this regular class and yields the linear onset in
Eq.~(12).
At long times $L(t)\to A$ and $R(t)\sim \lambda t$, so
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_2(t)\sim \frac{1}{\lambda t}.
\end{equation}
Differentiating the exact expression gives the peak condition
\begin{equation}
  R'(t)\left[L(t)-qR(t)\right]-2R(t)L'(t)=0.
\end{equation}
In the plateau regime this reduces to
\begin{equation}
  qR(t^\ast)=A,\qquad \alpha_2(t^\ast)=\frac{q}{4A}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, a measured peak height gives the plateau-regime estimates
\begin{equation}
  \frac{q}{A}\simeq 4\alpha_2(t^\ast),\qquad
  R(t^\ast)\simeq \frac{1}{4\alpha_2(t^\ast)}.
\end{equation}
For the delayed intensity used above, write
\begin{equation}
  R(t)=\lambda\tau_d F(t/\tau_d),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  F(s)=s-2(1-e^{-s})+\frac{1}{2}(1-e^{-2s}).
\end{equation}
If $\tau_d$ is obtained independently or fitted from the rise of the peak, the
peak time estimates the asymptotic renewal rate,
\begin{equation}
  \lambda \simeq
  \frac{1}{4\alpha_2(t^\ast)\tau_d F(t^\ast/\tau_d)}.
\end{equation}
The independent long-time check is $\lambda\simeq[t\,\alpha_2(t)]^{-1}$ once
$t$ is beyond the cage plateau and renewal delay.

\section{Self Van Hove Distribution}

The three-dimensional radial self van Hove distribution is a Poisson-weighted
Maxwell mixture,
\begin{equation}
  G_s(r,t)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty P[N(t)=n]
  \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\frac{r^2}{V_n(t)^{3/2}}
  \exp\left[-\frac{r^2}{2V_n(t)}\right],
\end{equation}
with $V_n(t)=L(t)+nq$. This representation shows directly why the distribution
has transient broad tails near the NGP peak and returns toward a matched
Gaussian baseline at long times.

\section{Discussion}

The model is not intended to replace molecular simulations or microscopic cage
jump analysis. Its role is to isolate a minimal mechanism: delayed renewal of
local cages creates a finite-time NGP peak while preserving ordinary long-time
Gaussian recovery. The delay time $\tau_d$ mainly controls when the peak
appears; the ratio $q/A$ controls its height in the plateau regime. This
separation of roles may be useful when interpreting short-time simulations or
coarse-grained cage-jump statistics near glass transition.
The generalized exponent argument also makes a falsifiable modeling
assumption explicit: a measured early-time NGP scaling
$\alpha_2(t)\propto t^\beta$ corresponds to an effective renewal-delay
exponent $m=\beta+1$ within this class. The square-delay model therefore
predicts an initially linear NGP rise, not merely a finite peak.

Several assumptions are deliberately restrictive. Renewal events are taken to
be Poisson once the delayed intensity is specified; cage-center jumps are
Gaussian and uncorrelated; and the local cage variance is reduced to a single
relaxation scale. These choices are what make the moment hierarchy and van Hove
mixture analytic. Their role is diagnostic rather than microscopic: correlated
jumps, broad waiting-time distributions, or non-Gaussian local cage motion would
modify the peak shape, but they would have to reproduce the same three
observables--the MSD plateau, the early-time NGP exponent, and the long-time
$1/t$ decay--to fall in the same renewal-cage universality class.

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} summarizes the distinction from nearby modeling
languages. The point is not that a renewal model is the only explanation of a
non-Gaussian peak. Rather, the delayed renewal construction supplies an
explicit bridge between a cage plateau, a finite-time NGP maximum, a radial van
Hove tail, the early-time exponent, and peak diagnostics that can be checked
against simulation trajectories.
